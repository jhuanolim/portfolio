## Overview

I'm starting a Singapore-based climbing shoe resole service built as a two-person family operation — my father handles production as the trained 
cobbler, and I run business development, marketing, and customer operations. We are training under a Barcelona-founded specialist, 
who is looking to educate cobblers in regions outside of Europe. 
 
The business targets Singapore's 20,000+ active climbers with a faster, more transparent, and more convenient resole service than anything currently 
available. At full capacity (150 pairs/month per cobbler), the business generates approximately SGD $9,750/month in revenue at a ~79% gross margin.

## Problem

Singapore's climbing community has effectively one specialist resole option: Stikngrip. The service works — they use quality rubber and have 
climbing-specific expertise — but the execution is poor. Turnaround runs 2+ months, communication goes silent for weeks at a time, and the intake 
process involves a Chrome-only Google Form. Community forums document consistent frustration.
 
The alternative, K Shoe & Bags, charges slightly lower prices but uses a generic cobbler approach that distorts downturned performance shoes and 
offers no drop-off network.
 
For a dedicated boulderer who burns through rubber every 6-9 months and wants their shoes back for next weekend's session, neither option is acceptable. 
The problem is not lack of demand — Stikngrip's 2-month backlog proves demand is real. The problem is that no one is serving it properly.

## Approach

The research process started with a bottom-up market sizing rather than top-down projections. Singapore has approximately 20–25 active climbing gyms 
with ~800 members each, yielding an estimated 18,000–22,000 active climbers. Of those, roughly 40–50% climb frequently enough to generate resole-relevant 
rubber wear, and after discounting for the large share of climbers who still discard worn shoes rather than resoling, the realistically accessible 
market today is approximately 350–670 pairs/month across all providers.
 
A community survey was deployed to validate demand, price sensitivity, and key objections. Stikngrip's service gaps were mapped systematically from 
forum posts, Reddit threads, and direct observation. The competitive landscape was modelled across three dimensions: turnaround time, logistics 
convenience, and digital experience.
 
For supply chain, we benchmarked against overseas operators — Black Rainbow Resoles in Las Vegas (150 pairs/month solo), BJR Retreads in NSW 
(Australia's largest by reputation), and Rock and Resole in Boulder, CO — to validate that 100–150 pairs/month is achievable as a solo full-time operation 
using batch-processing methods. We also invested early in a 3D printer to produce custom shoe lasts, addressing the most common failure mode of 
generic cobblers (geometric distortion of downturned shoes).
 
Training is being secured through our Barcelona Contact — positioning ourselves not as a student but as the Singapore node of their SEA expansion network.

## Key Insights

**Turnaround time is the primary purchase driver, not price.** Survey data and community feedback consistently point to Stikngrip's 2-month wait as 
the main reason climbers don't resole. Willingness to pay is not the constraint. A 3–4 week turnaround at the same price point is a meaningful 
differentiator, not a marginal one.
 
**The education gap is the larger long-term opportunity.** After discounting for awareness, approximately 60% of resole-eligible climbers in Singapore 
still throw away wearable shoes rather than resoling them. The addressable market roughly doubles if that behaviour shifts. Content marketing targeting 
the moment of first visible wear-through is the highest-leverage acquisition channel.
 
**Logistics friction kills repeat behaviour.** Climbers who tried resoling once and didn't return often cite inconvenience — not quality — as the reason. 
Solving drop-off and collection completely (gym boxes, 24/7 Pick Network lockers, door-to-door) is a prerequisite for strong retention, not a nice-to-have.
 
**The family business structure is a genuine operational advantage.** Separating production from customer operations from day one means the cobbler focuses 
entirely on craft while the business operator handles growth. This is the structure most mature resole operations identify as critical only after years of 
trying to do both.
